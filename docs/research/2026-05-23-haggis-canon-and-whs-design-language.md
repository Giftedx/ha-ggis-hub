## Part 1 — What haggis is (the food)

### Definition

> "Haggis is a savory pudding containing sheep's pluck (heart, liver, and
> lungs), minced with chopped onion, oatmeal, suet, seasonings, and
> salt, mixed with stock. The mixture is traditionally encased in the
> animal's stomach though artificial casings are now common."
> — [Wikipedia, _Haggis_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haggis)

Scottish Gaelic name: **_taigeis_** [ˈtʰakʲɪʃ].

### Ingredients (canonical recipe)

- Sheep's pluck — heart, liver, lungs (the offal in one parcel)
- Suet (hard kidney fat)
- Oatmeal (binds and gives the nutty texture)
- Onion, chopped
- Salt, black pepper, optional coriander / mace / nutmeg / cinnamon
- Stock and water
- Casing: **sheep's stomach** traditionally; **artificial casing** now common

### History (verified)

- **First written recipes** appear around **1430** in English cookbooks.
  The Lancashire verse cookbook _Liber Cure Cocorum_ records "**hagese**"
  made with offal and herbs.
- The Scottish poem _Flyting of Dunbar and Kennedy_ (before 1520)
  references "**haggeis**".
- Early printed recipe: Gervase Markham's 1615 _The English Huswife_ —
  "oat-meale mixed with blood, and the Liver…maketh that pudding which
  is called the Haggas."
- Three competing origin theories: **Scottish** (peasant practicality —
  way to use the cheapest offal), **Roman** (Alan Davidson — similar
  Roman products), and **Norse** (Clarissa Dickson Wright — "came to
  Scotland in a longship before Scotland was a single nation"; the
  "hag-" element traced by etymologist Walter William Skeat to Old
  Norse _haggw_ / Old Icelandic _hoggva_ = to hew/chop. Related Nordic
  dishes: Danish _hakkemad_, Norwegian _hakkemat_, Swedish _pölsa_).

### Appearance — verified from 21 reference photos

The earlier draft said "crumbly dark brown to almost-black mass."
That was lazy and wrong. After studying 21 photos covering haggis in
every state — raw, foil-boiled, whole on platter, piped in at Burns
night, cut open and bursting, crumbled on plate, sliced into discs
for breakfast, deep-fried in batter, gastropub tower, Tokyo
restaurant, pub toastie, canned, vegetarian, supermarket-packed,
chippy takeaway — the visual reality is much more varied. The whole
haggis is one thing; the interior revealed is another; the
supermarket disc is another; the chippy fritter is another. The hub
should render whichever of these reads most clearly at the relevant
scale and moment.

#### The whole haggis (intact, in its casing)

- **Shape:** irregular oval / spherical pouch — closest in form to a
  fat dumpling, a small American football, or a stuffed pillow. Not
  round-perfect. Natural folds and lumps where the casing is tied.
- **Size:** individual-portion haggis fits in a small dessert bowl
  ([refs/food-content-interior.jpg](refs/food-content-interior.jpg));
  a sharing-platter haggis is football-sized, large enough to be cut
  open and served from to a table
  ([refs/food-on-platter.jpg](refs/food-on-platter.jpg),
  [refs/food-cutting.jpg](refs/food-cutting.jpg)).
- **Casing colour range** (depends on cooking and recipe):
  - **Pale grey-beige** — lightly cooked, matte stomach-lining
    surface; looks almost like raw pastry dough or pale roast pork
    skin ([refs/food-cutting.jpg](refs/food-cutting.jpg),
    [refs/food-on-platter.jpg](refs/food-on-platter.jpg)).
  - **Glossy roast-brown** — well-cooked, juices on the surface;
    looks like a small glossy roast joint
    ([refs/food-content-interior.jpg](refs/food-content-interior.jpg)).
  - **Dark brown / nearly black** — heavily cooked, dense recipe, or
    aged natural casing; looks like a small dark grenade with a
    slightly leathery skin
    ([refs/food-great-chieftain.jpg](refs/food-great-chieftain.jpg),
    [refs/food-clapshot-bannocks-cheese.jpg](refs/food-clapshot-bannocks-cheese.jpg)).
- **Casing material:** traditional **sheep's stomach** gives a matte,
  mottled, folded look; commercial product uses **artificial sausage
  casing** which is smoother and more uniformly tubular
  ([refs/food-pudding-pack.jpg](refs/food-pudding-pack.jpg),
  [refs/food-macsween-vegetarian.jpg](refs/food-macsween-vegetarian.jpg)).
  Wikipedia: "traditionally encased in the animal's stomach though
  artificial casings are now common."
- **Raw casing detail (new):** [refs/food-extra-001.jpg](refs/food-extra-001.jpg)
  is the best single closeup of a raw whole haggis in hand — a sheep's
  stomach pouch shows **prominent white vein-and-membrane patterning**
  across a brown speckled surface. That web of pale veins across the
  brown is a strong recognition cue when the casing is traditional.

#### The interior (revealed when split open)

This is the visual most of the public actually associates with
"haggis as food", because the casing is usually cut off in the
kitchen or split open at the table.

- **Texture:** coarse, crumbly, distinctly grainy — **not** a smooth
  pâté. Individual **oatmeal grains** (golden-tan, pinhead size) sit
  in **brown minced offal** (looks like wet coarse beef mince).
  Closest familiar comparison: couscous mixed with cooked mince. The
  cleanest cross-section reference is the deep-fried Stromness
  takeaway ([refs/food-takeaway-stromness.jpg](refs/food-takeaway-stromness.jpg)).
- **Interior colour:**
  - **Light grey-brown to tan** when sliced into discs and pan-fried
    for breakfast — looks like coarse meatloaf or coarse pâté
    ([refs/food-scottish-breakfast.jpg](refs/food-scottish-breakfast.jpg)).
  - **Mid-brown wet mince** when scooped from a freshly opened
    haggis ([refs/food-whisky-added.jpg](refs/food-whisky-added.jpg),
    [refs/food-mash-tokyo.jpg](refs/food-mash-tokyo.jpg)).
  - **Dark brown crumb** when fully crumbled and re-served as a
    side mound ([refs/food-content-interior.jpg](refs/food-content-interior.jpg)).
- **Behaviour when cut:** crumbles. Does **not** slice cleanly like a
  sausage; falls apart into clumps and grains. The Burns-Night cut
  from end to end causes the interior to **burst out / spill
  forward** rather than slice
  ([refs/food-great-chieftain.jpg](refs/food-great-chieftain.jpg)).
- **Butcher's raw slices (new):** [refs/food-extra-050.jpg](refs/food-extra-050.jpg)
  shows pre-cut **raw butcher discs** on greaseproof paper — these
  hold their disc shape because the mince is cold and bound; brown-red
  meat with **highly visible golden-tan and brown oat grain** speckled
  through. The clearest single shot of the raw mince + oat texture in
  the corpus.
- **Per _Larousse Gastronomique_:** "an excellent nutty texture and
  delicious savoury flavour."

#### Burns Night plating — three distinct mounds

The canonical "haggis dinner" plate, repeated across most reference
photos, is three colour-distinct mounds on a white plate:

- **Haggis** — coarse brown mound on one side (mid-brown wet mince
  texture, or light grey-brown disc if pan-fried).
- **Neeps** — **mashed swede / rutabaga**, NOT mashed white turnip.
  Verified: in Scotland "neeps" = swede = the large yellow-fleshed
  rutabaga with purple skin (Swedish-developed 17th-century hybrid).
  The mash is **bright yellow-orange**, almost saffron — the
  brightest thing on the plate.
  Sources: [Scottish Scran](https://scottishscran.com/what-are-neeps/),
  [Scottish Mum](https://scottishmum.com/2020/02/what-is-the-difference-between-swede-neeps-and-turnip/),
  [Goodto Burns Night guide](https://www.goodto.com/food/food-news/what-are-neeps-all-you-need-to-know-about-the-traditional-burns-night-supper-dish-645701).
- **Tatties** — mashed potato, pure white, slightly fluffy mound.
- Optional: a **dram of whisky** poured over the haggis from a small
  silver cup ([refs/food-whisky-added.jpg](refs/food-whisky-added.jpg)),
  or sloshed into a tumbler beside the plate.
- Plate is usually plain white with a thin dark rim; sometimes on a
  **tartan tablecloth** or **yellow Burns napkin**.

This three-mound **brown / orange / white** composition is the
**single strongest visual signature** of haggis-as-food and must be
recognisable at a glance in any rendering.

#### Modern / gastropub variations

- **Stacked tower:** tatties at the base, neeps in the middle, haggis
  on top, garnished with crispy potato shavings + microgreens, with a
  whisky / peppercorn / cream sauce pooled around
  ([refs/food-tower-whiski-edinburgh.jpg](refs/food-tower-whiski-edinburgh.jpg),
  [refs/food-auld-smiddy-inn.jpg](refs/food-auld-smiddy-inn.jpg)).
- **Sauced puck:** a round haggis disc + scoops of neeps & tatties,
  all under a heavy beige whisky-cream sauce
  ([refs/food-cramond-inn.jpg](refs/food-cramond-inn.jpg)).
- **Burns-night home spread:** whole haggis + glass-dish clapshot
  (neeps & tatties mashed together) + round oat **bannocks** + wedge
  of fresh Scottish cheese (Crowdie or similar), on a wooden board
  ([refs/food-clapshot-bannocks-cheese.jpg](refs/food-clapshot-bannocks-cheese.jpg)).

#### Chippy / takeaway forms

- **Deep-fried "haggis supper":** battered ball or short fat cylinder,
  deep-fried golden, on chunky chips in a styrofoam / cardboard
  takeaway tray. Cut cross-section shows the coarse-grain brown mince
  interior inside the crispy golden batter — single best texture
  reference in the set
  ([refs/food-takeaway-stromness.jpg](refs/food-takeaway-stromness.jpg),
  [refs/food-supper-1.jpg](refs/food-supper-1.jpg)).
- **Haggis pakora** (Scottish-Indian, common in Glasgow): small
  golden-fried balls with chutney drizzle + yoghurt raita on a slate
  board with microherb garnish ([refs/food-pakora.jpg](refs/food-pakora.jpg)).
- **Haggis bites:** pub starter — golden-fried croquettes with sweet
  chilli dip and shredded salad
  ([refs/food-bites-with-sauce.jpg](refs/food-bites-with-sauce.jpg)).
- **Haggis toastie:** toasted-press sandwich, dark haggis filling
  visible at the edges ([refs/food-toastie-sandwich.jpg](refs/food-toastie-sandwich.jpg)).

#### Retail / packaged forms

Most Scots first encounter haggis as a supermarket product, not as a
ceremonial Burns dish. The hub should know these visual forms exist:

- **Hall's "Pudding Pack"** — black plastic fridge tray of pre-sliced
  PUCKS (haggis + black pudding + white pudding round discs), pale
  tan/grey with darker speckles, yellow + purple branded label. The
  supermarket-mass-market form
  ([refs/food-pudding-pack.jpg](refs/food-pudding-pack.jpg)).
- **Macsween "Vegetarian Haggis"** — smooth plastic-cased SAUSAGE log
  of vegetarian haggis (mushrooms, lentils, pulses, nuts). Pale
  grey-beige interior with darker speckles. Macsween brands themselves
  "Scotland's most loved." Wikipedia: vegetarian haggis first sold
  1984; now 25–40% of sales
  ([refs/food-macsween-vegetarian.jpg](refs/food-macsween-vegetarian.jpg)).
- **Canned tourist haggis** — pyramids of red+gold cans stacked into
  towers in Edinburgh Royal Mile tourist shops
  ([refs/food-edinburgh-royal-mile.jpg](refs/food-edinburgh-royal-mile.jpg)).
  Wikipedia: "sold in tins or a container which can be cooked in a
  microwave or conventional oven."

### Taste and texture

- Per the 2001 _Larousse Gastronomique_: "Although its description is
  not immediately appealing, haggis has an **excellent nutty texture
  and delicious savoury flavour**."
- Hearty, peppery, crumbly, oat-bound — closer to coarse mince than
  to smooth pâté.

### Preparation

- Traditionally **boiled** (inside the stomach casing) for several
  hours, often wrapped in foil to protect the casing in the pot
  ([refs/haggis-boiling.jpg](refs/haggis-boiling.jpg)).
- Modern variants: **baked** in the oven, **microwaved** (commercial
  microwaveable forms), **pan-fried in slices** (breakfast disc),
  **deep-fried** (chippy / pakora).

### Burns Night — what physically happens

Verified from [Wikipedia _Burns supper_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burns_supper).

1. **Procession** — a **bagpiper** leads, the cook carries the haggis
   behind on a large dish (typically a silver oval platter); **guests
   stand** as it enters. Common tunes: _A Man's A Man for A' That_,
   _Robbie Burns Medley_, _The Star O' Robbie Burns_.
2. **Address** — host or guest recites Burns's 1786 poem _Address to
   a Haggis_, opening with _"Fair fa' your honest, sonsie face, /
   Great chieftain o' the puddin-race!"_
3. **The cutting moment** — the knife specifically:
   - At **"His knife see rustic Labour dicht"** (= sharpens), the
     speaker draws and sharpens a knife (often a **dirk** or long
     bone-handled blade).
   - At **"An' cut you up wi' ready slicht"** (= with practiced
     skill), they **plunge it into the haggis and cut it open from
     end to end**. The casing splits, the coarse-grain interior
     bursts out onto the platter
     ([refs/food-cutting.jpg](refs/food-cutting.jpg),
     [refs/food-great-chieftain.jpg](refs/food-great-chieftain.jpg)).
4. **Toast** — whisky toast to the haggis precedes eating.
5. **Service order** — soup (Scotch broth / cock-a-leekie / cullen
   skink / potato) → **haggis with neeps and tatties** → cranachan or
   tipsy laird (whisky trifle) → oatcakes & cheese → whisky throughout.

### Commercial / modern variations

- **Vegetarian haggis** (first sold 1984) — pulses, nuts, mushrooms,
  carrots, lentils, oats, barley. **25–40% of sales today.**
- **Kosher haggis** — pork-free, conformant to Jewish dietary law.
- **Pakora**, **pizza topping**, **burger**, **sausage** all exist.

### Legal status

- The **US has banned import of haggis since 1971** because it contains
  sheep lung (~10–15% of traditional recipe). The ban on lung is
  separate from the 1989–2022 UK lamb/beef ban (lamb was lifted in
  2022; lung still banned).
- US-made "haggis" exists from Scottish-recipe US producers; it
  excludes sheep lung and uses artificial casings.

### Regional variants

- **Aberdeen northwards:** served with mince alongside the neeps and
  tatties.

## Part 2 — What the wild haggis is (the folklore creature)

### Definition

The **wild haggis** (humorously _Haggis scoticus_) is a **fictional
creature of Scottish folklore** invented as a tease for tourists who
don't realise the food is made from sheep offal. A **2003 survey by
haggis manufacturer Hall's of Broxburn found that 33% of US visitors to
Scotland believed the wild haggis to be a real animal.**

It is **small, furry, Highland-native**, with one famous anatomical
quirk:

### The uneven legs (the iconic feature)

> "The wild haggis's left and right legs are of different lengths…
> allowing it to run quickly around the steep mountains."

- **Clockwise variety:** longer **left** legs. Runs clockwise around a
  mountain (viewed from above).
- **Anti-clockwise variety:** longer **right** legs. Runs the other way.
- Per the joke, the two varieties **cannot interbreed** because males
  must face the same direction as females to mate, so they lose balance.
- "Caught by chasing them in the opposite direction; they cannot turn."

### Physical appearance — verified from museum taxidermies

The most-photographed reference is the **Kelvingrove Art Gallery and
Museum, Glasgow**, which displays a taxidermy wild haggis alongside a
real prepared haggis dish (cf.
[refs/wild-haggis-kelvingrove.jpg](refs/wild-haggis-kelvingrove.jpg)
and [refs/wild-haggis-kelvingrove-cropped.jpg](refs/wild-haggis-kelvingrove-cropped.jpg)).

Common features across the saved reference photos
([refs/wild-haggis-1.jpg](refs/wild-haggis-1.jpg),
[refs/wild-haggis-2.jpg](refs/wild-haggis-2.jpg),
[refs/wild-haggis-nah.jpg](refs/wild-haggis-nah.jpg)):

- **Body:** small, low-slung, four-legged. Body length roughly
  cat-to-badger sized. Shape closer to a **shaggy potato with a snout**
  than to a cartoon ball.
- **Fur:** **long shaggy mane** in ginger / blonde / grey along the top
  and sides — Highland-cow-of-the-smallholding vibes. Some specimens
  have a darker body underneath the mane.
- **Face:** **dark brown or near-black skin**, **pig-like or sloth-like
  snout that protrudes** from the face. The snout is a defining
  feature — it is **not** a button nose between the eyes.
- **Eyes:** small, deep-set, dark. Not the big anime-eyes of a
  stylised game sprite.
- **Ears:** small, pointed, upright (cat / fox proportions).
- **Legs:** four visible short stubby legs. Some specimens telegraph the
  uneven-length gag; most just show four short legs.
- **Habitat in displays:** sitting on a small mound of **pink/purple
  heather** — the moor plant. Sometimes paired with a real haggis
  pudding to land the joke.

This is **not the cute cartoon character** of stylised game art. The
taxidermies look more like museum chimaeras stitched from sheepskin,
fox, and pig parts than like an existing real animal.

### Related folklore creatures

The wild haggis sits in a family of comic impossible-anatomy beasts:

- **sidehill gouger** (US Appalachia)
- **dahu** (French / Swiss Alps)
- **jackalope** (US southwest)
- **wolpertinger** (Bavaria)
- **drop bear** (Australia)
- **tree octopus** (US Pacific Northwest)

All share the premise of "this anatomy can't exist; that's the joke."

### Museum displays

- **Kelvingrove Art Gallery and Museum**, Glasgow — the canonical
  reference. Taxidermy wild haggis with a real haggis pudding plated
  beside it, on pink heather.
- **Alternate museum specimens** — the bulk corpus
  ([refs/myth-005.jpg](refs/myth-005.jpg) and others in the
  `myth-*.jpg` set) shows variations on the same staging pattern from
  other museums: a small four-legged shaggy mammal posed on **pink
  heather**, next to a real haggis pudding as visual punchline, with
  a "Please do not touch" sign. The Kelvingrove staging is the
  archetype but not the only one.

### Modern "what if the wild haggis were real" renderings

A significant subset of contemporary wild-haggis imagery (especially
from the **Haggis Wildlife Foundation** brand ecosystem) consists of
**AI-generated photoreal renderings** — beautiful, painterly photos
of a creature that has never existed, rendered as if photographed in
the wild. These are not historical canon but they ARE the modern
public-facing visual interpretation of the myth and the hub may
borrow from them as a *register reference* (without copying them):

- A shaggy-wool sheep-meets-Highland-cow creature with dark snout,
  small ears, and four short visible legs, stood on Highland moss
  ([refs/myth-020.jpg](refs/myth-020.jpg)).
- A wet beige spiky-fur creature with claws standing on a stream
  rock ([refs/myth-030.jpg](refs/myth-030.jpg)).
- Editorial group shots of guinea-pig-like fuzzy creatures with
  shaggy ginger fur and "Wild Haggis Animals" titling
  ([refs/myth-100.jpg](refs/myth-100.jpg)).

These tell us **the modern brand interpretation** is fluffier, rounder,
warmer, and cuter than the chimera-stitched museum taxidermies. The
hub picking between "museum-taxidermy-realism" and "AI-photoreal-cute"
is a register decision; both are valid in the canon family.

### Merch / plush interpretations

The myth is monetised through plush toys, postcards, soft toys, and
gift items. The plush interpretation
([refs/myth-050.jpg](refs/myth-050.jpg)) is **round, earthy-orange,
big-eyed, dark-snouted, cream-bellied** — closer to a chubby teddy
than to the museum-chimera. The hub should know this is the version
visitors are most likely to have *touched in a shop* and may be a
useful aesthetic reference if going storybook-cute.

## Part 3 — Implications for the hub's visual language

### The hub is its own project

The hub is **a separate product** from any individual haggis-themed game.
Its job is to be the cohesive **lobby** that lets a visitor walk the
hub's haggis mascot up to whichever game they want. It has its own:

- visual language
- typography
- voice and copy register
- composition and mood
- aesthetic genre

It is **NOT** required to copy the design tokens of any individual
game in the family. It must only **be visibly part of the same world**
— meaning rooted in the same canon (the food, the creature, the
Highland setting) so visitors recognise it as "the haggis-games place".

### Where the hub shares with the games

Across every haggis-themed game and the hub:

- **The haggis joke is the protagonist family.** WHS uses a stylised
  wild-haggis creature. The hub uses **The Wee Chieftain**, a living
  whole-haggis food mascot. Future games can choose their own rendering
  if they preserve the shared joke and readable haggis-family cues
  (Highland context, uneven-leg gag when useful, oats/food/casing or
  creature anatomy as appropriate).
- **Setting is Scotland / the Highlands.** Heather, peat, stone,
  bothies, lochs, glens, mist, dawn/dusk, tartan, whisky, oats.
- **Voice has a Scots register option.** Standard English is fine;
  Scots-tint and full Scots are also valid for any project in the
  family.
- **Names follow the haggis joke.** `ha + ggis = haggis`. The domain
  pun is part of the brand at the hub level.

### Where the hub can (and should) differ from any specific game

Everything else is up for grabs. The hub may pick:

- a **different time of day** (dawn vs WHS's dusk)
- a **different illustrative register** (storybook / painterly /
  low-fi-pixel / line-drawn / collage) so it doesn't look like a clone
  of any one game
- a **different palette** drawn from real haggis canon, e.g.:
  - **heather purple** `#7a4a9c`-ish (the moor where the creature lives)
  - **neeps orange** `#e4a020`-ish (mashed swede / rutabaga on the Burns plate — NOT white turnip)
  - **tatties cream** `#f0e6c8`-ish (mashed potato)
  - **whisky amber** `#c8842a`-ish (the dram)
  - **oat tan** `#c4a878`-ish (the binder grain of the food)
  - **peat brown** `#3a2418`-ish (the food, the floor, the creature's
    underbody)
  - **tartan accents** (red / green / blue thin lines) for occasional
    decoration
- a **different typography family** so the hub feels its own
- a **different mood** — the hub is a welcoming lobby, not a tense
  combat scene; it can be warmer, calmer, and slower than WHS

### What the current hub state gets wrong (post-correction)

The previous iteration of this doc told me to adopt the WHS DESIGN.md
palette and monospace typography wholesale. That was an over-correction.
The hub should now **back out of the verbatim WHS clone** and develop
its own voice that's still canon-grounded:

- Pick a hub-specific palette from the real haggis canon list above —
  not WHS's `#1a1a2e` / `#d4a017`.
- Pick a hub-specific typography (a humanist serif, a hand-drawn-feel
  sans, or a textured display face) — not WHS's monospace.
- Keep the hub player visually recognisable as a haggis-family mascot,
  but let it be food-shaped first rather than a snouted creature. WHS
  is the readability reference, not the required anatomy.
- Voice can stay Scots-tinted but should not copy WHS's exact copy
  patterns.

### Recommended hub direction

**Highland dawn storybook bothy.** A small Scottish cottage interior at
first light, painted-illustration register, warm dawn light through a
small window. The Wee Chieftain, the hub's living whole-haggis food
mascot, has just woken up beside the embers of last night's fire and is
choosing which door (which game) to step out into today. Palette pulls
from neeps orange + heather purple + tatties cream + peat brown + oat
tan, with a dedicated cooked-casing ramp for the mascot. Typography is
a humanist serif with a modest hand-drawn feel for door signs. Voice is
Scots-tinted but warmer and slower than WHS's combat banter.

This direction:

- Is **distinct** from WHS (which is dusk / pixel-art bullet hell /
  monospace / Scots-combat-banter).
- Is **rooted in canon** (Highland bothy, heather, peat, the haggis
  food joke, the uneven-leg gag, Burns-plate palette).
- Reads as **a lobby** (calm, welcoming, intentional) rather than as
  another game.
- Scales — additional games slot in as additional doors in the same
  bothy, each with its own sign / glyph / hint of its style.

Anchor this direction in the next implementation slice. Reject any
further "make it look more like WHS" pressure as off-brief.
